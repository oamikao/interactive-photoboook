History of Internet

April 22, 2026

A topic of the web that stood out to me is the contrast of social media being algorithm-based (user is suggested a wide variety of content), and chatrooms being subject-specific (user chooses what to see). I think it's an interesting observation, and it may be tied to how different the two mediums of communication are. Chatrooms are very minimalistic and straightforward, where conversations exist within certain topics. The idea itself works due to its dedicated “rooms”, whereas being suggested topics steers away from the idea and starts to lean more into a ‘browser’ or the beginnings of a social media site.

An algorithm based chatroom wouldn’t entirely make sense, as algorithms are mainly used for sorting data and ranking content at a speed and scale that would be impossible for humans, so in a space where “content” doesn’t really exist outside of chats, there isn’t much to be sorted through and suggested to users. Another observation to make is why users choose one over the other. Social media platforms do an excellent job of suggesting anything and everything, a medium of exploration that I like to use as well, so an algorithm boosts the site's purpose. In chatrooms, many users intend to find a topic they like and discuss that without the hassle of being bombarded with irrelevant stuff, so an algorithm would not be beneficial.

Algorithms aren't always perfect though, and it can often ruin a users experience, so it would definitely be interesting to see how social media without algorithms would operate (maybe like a glorified chatroom)?
